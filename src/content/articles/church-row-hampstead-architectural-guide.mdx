Church Row is often described as the finest Georgian street in London. This is not hyperboleâ€”it is the assessment of architectural historians, conservation officers, and the Hampstead Conservation Area Advisory Committee. The street's unbroken terrace of early-eighteenth-century houses, its spatial relationship to St John-at-Hampstead, and its remarkable state of preservation make it an object lesson in Georgian urban planning.

For homeowners fortunate enough to own property on Church Row, this heritage brings both privilege and responsibility. This guide examines the street's architectural character, identifies renovation precedents, and outlines the planning framework that governs any alterations.

## Historical Development

Church Row was laid out in the 1720s as part of Hampstead's first significant expansion from medieval village to fashionable spa town. The street was designed to connect the parish church (St John-at-Hampstead, built 1745â€“47 on the site of an earlier medieval church) with Hampstead High Street, creating a formal approach that announced the village's aspirations to Georgian sophistication.

### The Original Development (1720â€“1730)

The south side of Church Row was built first, with most houses dating to 1720â€“1725. These are three-storey brown brick terraces with:

- **Flemish bond brickwork** (alternating headers and stretchers in each course)
- **Rubbed brick flat arches** over windows with gauged voussoirs
- **Timber sash windows** in the original six-over-six configuration
- **Panelled front doors** with rectangular fanlights (not the later semicircular type)
- **Cast-iron railings** to basement areas (mostly Victorian replacements of original)
- **Parapet rooflines** concealing shallow pitched roofs behind

The north side was developed slightly later (1725â€“1735), with slightly larger plots and more elaborate doorcase detailing. Several houses on this side have full-width pedimented doorcasesâ€”a mark of status that the earlier south-side houses lack.

### Later Modifications

Unlike many Georgian streets that suffered Victorian alterations or twentieth-century neglect, Church Row remained largely intact due to:

1. **Continuous occupation by wealthy families**: The street never declined into multi-occupation or commercial use, avoiding the subdivisions and shopfront insertions that disfigured many Georgian terraces.

2. **Early conservation awareness**: Hampstead residents were active in the Victorian preservation movement. The Hampstead Antiquarian and Historical Society (founded 1897) documented and advocated for the village's historic fabric decades before statutory listing existed.

3. **Conservation Area designation (1968)**: Church Row was included in the original Hampstead Conservation Area, one of the first designated under the Civic Amenities Act 1967.

4. **Listing (1950sâ€“1970s)**: Every house on Church Row is Grade II or Grade II* listed, providing statutory protection against inappropriate alterations.

## Architectural Analysis

### Facade Composition

Church Row exemplifies the Georgian principle of **unity in variety**. The terrace reads as a single compositionâ€”consistent heights, materials, and rhythmâ€”yet each house possesses subtle individuality through doorcase design, railings, and internal arrangement.

The facade proportions follow classical ratios:
- Window widths approximately 1:2 ratio (width to height) for sashes
- Floor-to-ceiling heights diminishing upward (tallest ground floor, shortest second floor)
- Ground-floor windows taller than those above, with first-floor slightly shorter, second-floor smallest

This hierarchy reflects both classical convention and practical function: principal reception rooms on the ground and first floors received the grandest treatment; bedrooms above were more modest.

### Materials

**Brickwork**: The brown London stock bricks, hand-made with distinctive color variation, have weathered to a warm palette of umber, ochre, and rose. Modern Ibstock or Michelmersh bricks can approximate this appearance, but truly matching repairs require reclaimed stocks from contemporary buildings.

**Lime mortar**: Original pointing was in lime mortarâ€”soft, permeable, and able to accommodate the slight movement inherent in old structures. Much Victorian and twentieth-century repointing used hard Portland cement, which traps moisture and causes brick decay. Any facade repairs should remove cement pointing and reinstate lime.

**Joinery**: Original sash windows were in softwood (Baltic pine or similar) with lead paint. Most surviving joinery is nineteenth-century replacement (Georgian sashes had shorter lifespans due to less durable timber and paint). Appropriate repairs use kiln-dried softwood in traditional profiles, painted with linseed oil paint.

**Ironwork**: The wrought-iron railings and boot scrapers are mostly Victorian (the originals would have been plain bars; the decorative patterns indicate 1840sâ€“1860s replacement). Where railings survive, they should be maintained; where removed (often in WWII salvage drives), reinstatement requires Cast Iron Restoration or similar specialists.

### Interior Character

Behind the Georgian facades, interiors vary considerably. Some houses retain:

- Original panelled entrance halls and staircase compartments
- Cornices and ceiling roses (plainer than Victorianâ€”typically simple egg-and-dart or dentil mouldings)
- Six-panel doors with H-L hinges
- Wooden chimneypieces with bolection mouldings (early Georgian) or architrave surrounds (later Georgian)
- Original joists, floorboards, and lime plaster on lath

Others have been comprehensively altered internallyâ€”particularly during 1960sâ€“1980s renovations when conservation sensibility was less developed. These houses present opportunities for sympathetic restoration, reinstating period detailing where it has been lost.

## Planning Framework

### Conservation Area Status

Church Row lies within the Hampstead Conservation Area. Under the Camden Local Plan and the NPPF, development must:

- **Preserve or enhance** the character and appearance of the conservation area
- **Respect established building lines**, heights, massing, and materials
- **Avoid harm** to the significance of heritage assets

### Listed Building Controls

Every house is individually listed (Grade II or Grade II* at Nos. 12â€“14). This means:

- **Listed Building Consent (LBC)** is required for any works affecting the building's special interestâ€”including internal alterations
- **No works to the exterior** (including painting, repairs, or window replacement) should proceed without confirming LBC is not required
- **Enforcement action** can be taken against unauthorized works, including requirements to reverse alterations

### Typical Applications

**Usually Approved** (with appropriate detailing):
- Like-for-like repairs using traditional materials
- Removal of inappropriate additions (e.g., satellite dishes, plastic rainwater goods)
- Internal alterations not affecting principal rooms or historic fabric
- Rear extensions where precedent exists and impact is contained

**Usually Refused**:
- Rooflights visible from Church Row (the parapet roofline is a defining characteristic)
- Replacement windows in uPVC or non-traditional profiles
- Basement excavations involving front lightwells or visible changes to street level
- Satellite dishes or air conditioning units on street-facing elevations
- Modern infill extensions at second-floor level where the roofline is exposed

### Working with Camden Planning

Pre-application advice is essential before any significant project on Church Row. Camden's Conservation & Design Team can indicate likely constraints and may refer applications to the Hampstead Conservation Area Advisory Committee (CAAC) for comment.

The CAAC meets monthly and reviews applications affecting the conservation area's character. While advisory, their comments carry weight with planning officers. Applicants benefit from early engagementâ€”the CAAC prefers to guide proposals rather than object to them retrospectively.

**Approach**: The extension was designed as a clearly contemporary interventionâ€”full-height glazing, zinc cladding, minimal structureâ€”rather than a pastiche Georgian addition. ### No. 31: Basement Excavation (Refused, 2018)

**Scope**: Full basement excavation beneath the garden footprint; new basement accommodation including media room, gym, and plant room; front lightwell.

**Approach**: The application proposed a front lightwell to provide natural light and ventilation to the basementâ€”a common feature in Victorian and Edwardian conversions but alien to the Georgian character of Church Row.

**Outcome**: Refused on grounds that the front lightwell would harm the street's visual continuity (no house on Church Row has a visible basement lightwell) and that excavation beneath the garden would impact mature trees. An appeal was dismissed.

**Lesson**: Basement excavations on Church Row face significant resistance. Where approved (e.g., No. 16 in 2016), the scheme was strictly limited to below-building footprint with no visible external changes.

## Interior Design Considerations

For owners seeking to renovate interiors while respecting the Georgian character:

### Colours

Georgian interiors were not the uniform off-white of National Trust properties. Historical paint analysis reveals:

- **Strong colours**: Prussian blue, sage green, terracotta, stone grey
- **Contrast**: Dark woodwork (dados, doorcases) against lighter walls, or vice versa
- **Hierarchy**: Principal rooms in richer tones; service areas plain distemper

Little Greene's Georgian palette is based on period research and includes Church Row-appropriate options: *Invisible Green*, *Pale Lupin*, *Stone-Pale-Warm*.

### Flooring

Original floors were likely wide pine boards, painted or bare. Later Georgian houses might have had stone flagstones in halls. Appropriate replacements include:

- Reclaimed wide pine boards (if originals lost)
- Yorkstone flagstones in entrance halls (if evidence of historic use)
- Engineered oak in herringbone for principal rooms (a sympathetic contemporary choice)

### Lighting

Georgian houses relied on candles and later gaslight. The shift to electric lighting should preserve the sense of atmosphere:

- Avoid downlighters (they create harsh shadows and damage ceilings)
- Use table lamps, wall sconces, and pendant fittings for ambient light
- Consider dimmer switches to adjust intensity
- Picture lights for artwork, discreet floor uplighters for architectural emphasis

### Kitchens and Bathrooms

Modern requirements need not compromise historic character:

- **Kitchens**: Freestanding furniture (dressers, tables) is more appropriate than fitted units; or use fitted cabinetry designed as discrete built-in furniture with frame-and-panel detailing
- **Bathrooms**: Console basins, high-level cisterns, and cast-iron baths suit the period; wet rooms and wall-hung sanitaryware do not
